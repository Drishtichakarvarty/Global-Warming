What is Global Warming?

Global warming refers to the long-term increase in Earth's average surface temperature due to human activities, primarily the release of greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrous oxide (N2O). These gases trap heat in the atmosphere, creating a "greenhouse effect" that leads to rising temperatures.

The phenomenon of global warming is not new. The Earth's climate has naturally fluctuated over geological timescales due to factors such as volcanic activity, changes in solar radiation, and natural greenhouse gas concentrations. However, the rapid increase in global temperatures observed over the past century is largely attributed to human activities since the Industrial Revolution. The widespread use of fossil fuels for energy, industrial processes, and deforestation has significantly increased the concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, driving unprecedented warming.

Global warming poses significant risks to human health. Higher temperatures can lead to heat-related illnesses and deaths, particularly among vulnerable populations such as the elderly and those with pre-existing health conditions. The changing climate also affects the distribution and prevalence of vector-borne diseases, such as malaria and dengue fever, as warmer temperatures expand the habitats of mosquitoes and other disease carriers. Additionally, increased air pollution from higher temperatures can exacerbate respiratory conditions like asthma.
